1. DP VS BUFFERLEVEL BEHAVIOUR AND WHEN DP PROVES GOOD
======================================================================

Main observation:
DP does not mainly improve performance in the low-load region. At low PIR, the network is lightly congested, so both DP and bufferlevel selection behave similarly. DP becomes useful when the NoC approaches the congestion knee, where local queue-state selection is no longer sufficient.

Reason:
Bufferlevel is a local heuristic. It chooses based on immediate neighbouring buffer state. This is effective when congestion is weak or spatially shallow, but it can fail near the knee because a locally free direction may still lead into a globally congested region.

DP is a multi-hop cost-to-go metric. It propagates a congestion-aware cost field over the NoC, so the selection unit can avoid directions that are locally acceptable but globally expensive.

Final confirmed 5x5x3 full sweep, CINTERVAL = DP_CYCLE:

PIR      BL delay   DP delay   DP delay reduction   DP wins   Throughput change   Energy change
0.016    20.72      18.99      8.35%                3/3       +0.02%              -2.22%
0.018    26.72      21.86      18.18%               3/3       +0.18%              -3.82%
0.020    37.03      25.38      31.46%               3/3       +0.02%              -5.44%
0.025    454.33     97.78      78.48%               3/3       +2.20%              -4.71%
0.030    1535.84    1263.67    17.72%               3/3       +2.40%              -0.36%
0.035    2386.29    2157.06    9.61%                3/3       +1.23%              +0.01%
0.040    3107.29    2953.26    4.96%                3/3       +1.21%              +0.35%

Interpretation:
- 0.016-0.020: low-to-moderate congestion. DP advantage increases gradually.
- 0.025: congestion knee. DP gives its strongest benefit.
- 0.030-0.040: deep saturation. DP still wins all seeds, but both policies are overloaded, so the relative improvement shrinks.

Key result:
At PIR = 0.025 for 5x5x3:
BL delay = 454.33
DP delay = 97.78
DP delay reduction = 78.48%
DP wins = 3/3 seeds

Reassurance rerun:
A later 5x5x3 rerun reproduced the same values up to rounding. This confirms that the known good DP behaviour is stable and that no later code/settings change broke the 5x5x3 reference case.

Conclusion for point 1:
DP proves good around the congestion knee. It is less important at low load and less dominant in deep saturation. Its value is strongest when congestion is forming but has not yet overwhelmed the network.

The 5x5x4 case shows lower improvement because the fourth z-layer appears to soften the congestion knee for this configuration. The additional layer gives more spatial spreading and more vertical path diversity, so bufferlevel does not collapse as sharply. Therefore, the relative DP improvement is lower even though DP still wins.

Conclusion for point 3:
DP is effective across different NoC configurations, but the magnitude of improvement depends on topology, z dimension, traffic pressure, and where the selected PIR sits relative to the knee. The correct conclusion is not that DP always scales monotonically better with size. The correct conclusion is that DP is a valid multi-hop congestion-aware baseline whose advantage appears when local bufferlevel decisions become insufficient.

5. Z-PARITY, Z-DEPTH, AND 3D ASPECT-RATIO FINDINGS
======================================================================

Initial hypothesis:
The lower DP improvement in 5x5x4 compared with 5x5x3 may be related to the even number of z-planes. In oddevenbalanced routing, adjacent planes alternate orientation. With an odd number of planes, one orientation has majority representation; with an even number of planes, OE0/OE1 are equally represented.

Updated evidence:
The later 4x4x3, 4x4x4, 4x4x5, and 6x6x4 results show that z parity alone is not enough to explain DP behaviour.

DP benefit therefore depends on 3D aspect ratio and congestion structure, not z parity alone.

6. MILESTONE RESULTS AND SIGNIFICANT FINDINGS
======================================================================

Milestone A - DP implementation corrected:
Several implementation problems in the DP selection unit were identified and corrected:
- rx_dp_cost was uninitialised in dpProcess.
- selectionDP had an unconditional push_back that discarded DP rankings under contention.
- routing_directionsUpdater had reset-ordering and no_dst issues.
- routing_directionsUpdater was changed to clock.pos() for deterministic latching.
- cost_mem[DPSIZE][DIRECTIONS] was added for coherent per-destination Bellman relaxation.
- frozen_local_cost was introduced at pass boundaries.
- A per-hop term was added so cost-to-go includes path length, not congestion only.

Significance:
The DP unit became a valid and testable multi-hop baseline rather than a broken or unstable policy.

Milestone B - Turn-law-aware DP relaxation:
The DP relaxation was made aware of the balanced odd-even turn law through can_turnOddEvenBalanced. This prevents Bellman propagation from considering illegal routing turns.

Significance:
The cost field is now consistent with the actual deadlock-free routing law.

Milestone C - Thermal-management regression resolved:
Disabling the dy_thermal_manage SC_METHOD caused max/min counters to stay zero. Combined with gamma defaulting to 1.0, cost_to_go wrote constant cost values. Thermal management was confirmed inert when dy_t_mode = 0, so the SC_METHOD should not be disabled.

Significance:
This restored congestion awareness in the DP cost computation.

Milestone D - CINTERVAL issue diagnosed:
For small 5x5x3 NoCs, CINTERVAL = DP_CYCLE/4 was too short and produced inconsistent results. Returning to CINTERVAL = DP_CYCLE recovered coherent behaviour and matched earlier clean results.

At PIR = 0.025:
Earlier clean 5x5x3:
BL delay approximately 507.45
DP delay approximately 100.38

Current corrected 5x5x3 with CINTERVAL = DP_CYCLE:
BL delay = 454.33
DP delay = 97.78

Significance:
The inconsistency was due mainly to CINTERVAL/statistical sampling effects, not the normalized cost-to-go formulation.

Milestone E - Final 5x5x3 knee result:
At PIR = 0.025:
BL delay = 454.33
DP delay = 97.78
DP delay reduction = 78.48%
DP wins = 3/3 seeds

Significance:
This is the strongest clean Stage 1 evidence that DP can outperform bufferlevel near the congestion knee.

Milestone F - Buffer-size sensitivity:
DP remains better across buffer sizes 8, 16, and 32. At PIR = 0.025:
Buffer 8: 73.28% delay reduction
Buffer 16: 59.74% delay reduction
Buffer 32: 55.85% delay reduction

Significance:
The DP benefit is robust across buffer depths and is not restricted to one buffer configuration.

Milestone G - Routing substrate result:
Plain oddeven enters congestion earlier than oddevenbalanced in 5x5x3. Oddevenbalanced improves both bufferlevel and DP absolute delay. The best combination is oddevenbalanced + DP.

Significance:
The balanced routing method is not merely a neutral substrate; it improves the routing baseline itself.

Milestone H - NoC-size/configuration comparison:
Representative knee improvements:

Significance:
DP improvement is not universal or monotonic with NoC size. It depends on how each configuration enters congestion. This is an important scientific finding because it prevents overclaiming.

Milestone I - 3D aspect-ratio finding:
The z=4 sequence shows improvement increasing as x-y width grows:
4x4x4: 16.36%
5x5x4: 33.39%
6x6x4: 55.83%

Significance:
The issue is not simply even z or z=4. DP benefits more when the x-y plane is wide enough to provide usable horizontal path diversity. Narrow, z-heavy topologies can restrict the effective alternatives available to DP.

FULL UPDATED STAGE 1 CONCLUSION
======================================================================

Stage 1 can be concluded as a validation and baseline-characterisation stage.

The simulator now includes a corrected deadlock-free balanced odd-even routing scheme and a corrected distributed DP selection unit. The DP unit performs turn-law-aware Bellman-style cost propagation and uses a normalized congestion-aware cost-to-go with a hop component. The most important implementation bugs that previously invalidated DP behaviour have been identified and fixed.

The experimental evidence shows that DP selection is a valid multi-hop congestion-aware baseline. Its strongest advantage appears near the congestion knee, where local bufferlevel selection can make poor decisions because it sees only immediate queue state. Around the knee, DP can substantially reduce average delay by using a propagated cost field that captures downstream congestion.

The final 5x5x3 run with CINTERVAL = DP_CYCLE gives the clearest repeatable result:
At PIR = 0.025, bufferlevel delay is 454.33 cycles, while DP delay is 97.78 cycles, corresponding to a 78.48% delay reduction. DP wins all three seeds at this point, and a later rerun reproduced the same result.

The 8x8x3 configuration confirms that this behaviour can also appear in a larger NoC. At PIR = 0.016, DP reduces delay from 458.68 to 130.60 cycles, a 71.53% reduction.

The buffer-depth sweep confirms that the DP benefit is not limited to a single buffer size. DP remains better under buffer sizes 8, 16, and 32, although the relative improvement decreases when larger buffers absorb some congestion.

The routing comparison confirms that oddevenbalanced is a better routing substrate than plain oddeven for the tested 5x5x3 transpose workload. Plain oddeven enters congestion earlier, while oddevenbalanced reduces absolute delay for both bufferlevel and DP. The best tested combination is oddevenbalanced + DP.

The topology comparison gives the most important scientific nuance. DP does not improve uniformly across all NoC sizes. Some configurations are strongly DP-friendly, such as 5x5x3, 4x4x3, 6x6x4, and 8x8x3. Other configurations, especially 4x4x4 and 4x4x5, show weak or unstable DP benefit.

The earlier hypothesis that odd/even z-plane parity alone controls DP behaviour is not supported. 4x4x3 is strong, but 4x4x5 is weak even though both have odd z. The better explanation is a 3D aspect-ratio and congestion-structure effect. When z increases while x and y remain narrow, the topology can become vertically constrained or less favourable to DP's cost-to-go decisions. When the x-y plane widens while z remains fixed, DP benefit increases again, as shown by the z=4 sequence:
4x4x4 -> 16.36%
5x5x4 -> 33.39%
6x6x4 -> 55.83%

The correct scientific claim for Stage 1 is therefore:

Corrected DP selection is a valid and effective multi-hop congestion-aware baseline. It can significantly outperform bufferlevel near the congestion knee, but its advantage is not universal, not monotonic with NoC size, and not guaranteed to grow automatically with dimensionality. Its benefit depends on routing substrate, network configuration, buffer size, 3D aspect ratio, traffic pressure, and the position of the operating point relative to the congestion knee.

The negative finding is also valuable:
Stage 1 does not prove that hand-tuned DP universally scales better than bufferlevel. Instead, it establishes DP and bufferlevel as characterised, valid baselines and identifies when DP is most useful.

Stage 1 is therefore sufficiently concluded.
The recommended next step is Stage 2: DNN traffic trace generation.
Future RL stages can use these results as a baseline and investigate whether learned value functions can provide a stronger adaptive multi-hop metric than the hand-tuned DP cost field, especially in configurations where DP reacts late, is constrained by topology, or loses effectiveness in deep saturation.
